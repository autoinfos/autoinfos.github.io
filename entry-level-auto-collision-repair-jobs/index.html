Entry Level Auto Collision Repair Jobs Get Your Foot In The Door

Written by Luffy Oct 25, 2023 Â· 4 min read

Have you been searching for a career that offers hands-on work, problem solving, and a steady paycheck? Look no further than entry level auto collision repair jobs. While the field may not be glamorous, it offers a stable and rewarding career path for those willing to put in the work.

Entry level auto collision repair jobs can be physically demanding and require attention to detail, but they also offer the opportunity to work with cutting-edge technology and learn valuable skills that can be applied to other industries.

If you're interested in starting a career in auto collision repair, there are a few things you should know. First, entry level positions typically require a high school diploma or equivalent, and may require completion of a vocational or technical program. Additionally, you may need to obtain certification from the National Institute for Automotive Service Excellence (ASE).

In this article, we'll explore the ins and outs of entry level auto collision repair jobs, including the necessary education and training, the job outlook, and the potential for advancement.

What Are Entry Level Auto Collision Repair Jobs?

Entry level auto collision repair jobs involve repairing vehicles that have been damaged in accidents. This can include repairing or replacing body panels, frame straightening, and painting. In addition to hands-on work, entry level positions may also involve customer service, such as providing estimates and explaining repairs to clients.

My personal experience with entry level auto collision repair jobs began when I was fresh out of high school and looking for a career path. I had always enjoyed working with my hands, and I knew I wanted a job that offered variety and challenge. After completing a vocational program in auto collision repair, I landed an entry level position at a local body shop. While the work was physically demanding at times, I found it incredibly rewarding to see a damaged vehicle transformed into its pre-accident condition.

What Education and Training Are Required?

While a high school diploma or equivalent is typically required for entry level positions, vocational or technical training is highly recommended. Many community colleges and vocational schools offer programs in auto collision repair, which can range from six months to two years in length. These programs typically cover a range of topics, including welding, painting, and body repair. Additionally, certification from the National Institute for Automotive Service Excellence (ASE) is often required or preferred by employers.

What Is the Job Outlook for Entry Level Auto Collision Repair Jobs?

The job outlook for entry level auto collision repair jobs is promising. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, employment of automotive body and glass repairers is projected to grow 4 percent from 2019 to 2029, about as fast as the average for all occupations. As the number of vehicles on the road continues to increase, so too will the demand for skilled auto collision repair technicians.

How Can You Advance in Your Career?

Advancement in the field of auto collision repair typically involves gaining experience and additional training. Experienced technicians may move into supervisory or management roles, while others may choose to specialize in specific areas of repair, such as frame or paint work. Additionally, obtaining additional certifications from ASE can increase your knowledge and earning potential.

Is Entry Level Auto Collision Repair Right for You?

If you enjoy working with your hands, solving problems, and have a keen eye for detail, entry level auto collision repair jobs may be a great fit for you. While the work can be physically demanding, it offers a stable and rewarding career path with opportunities for advancement.

Question and Answer

Q: What skills are necessary for entry level auto collision repair jobs?
A: Entry level auto collision repair jobs require attention to detail, physical dexterity, problem-solving skills, and customer service skills.

Q: What is the average salary for entry level auto collision repair jobs?
A: According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the median annual wage for automotive body and glass repairers was $44,550 in May 2019.

Q: Is certification from the National Institute for Automotive Service Excellence (ASE) required for entry level auto collision repair jobs?
A: While certification is not always required, it is often preferred by employers and can increase your earning potential.

A: Employment of automotive body and glass repairers is projected to grow 4 percent from 2019 to 2029, about as fast as the average for all occupations.

Conclusion of Entry Level Auto Collision Repair Jobs

Entry level auto collision repair jobs may not be the most glamorous, but they offer a stable and rewarding career path for those willing to put in the work. With the right education and training, you can gain the skills necessary to succeed in this growing field, and potentially advance to higher-paying supervisory or management roles. So if you're looking for a career that offers hands-on work, problem solving, and a steady paycheck, consider entry level auto collision repair jobs.
